Asthma is a long-term condition that affects the airways in the lungs. During flare-ups, the airways become inflamed and narrower, which makes it harder to move air out when breathing out.

Common asthma symptoms include wheezing, coughing, shortness of breath, and chest tightness. Symptoms often come and go. They may get worse with viral infections, exercise, allergies, cold air, laughter, or crying. Symptoms are often worse at night or early in the morning.

Asthma has no cure, but it can usually be managed. Management commonly involves avoiding triggers, following an asthma action plan, and using prescribed medicines. People with asthma should talk with a clinician about a plan for day-to-day control and what to do if symptoms worsen.

Signs that asthma may be getting worse include having symptoms more often than usual, needing reliever medicine more often than usual, waking at night because of coughing or breathing symptoms, or having trouble doing usual activities because of breathing problems.

This document is appropriate for patient-education questions about what asthma is, common symptoms, trigger patterns, and the idea of action-plan-based management.
