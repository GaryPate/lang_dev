# Flu Treatment

Most people with flu have mild illness and do not need antiviral treatment or other medical care beyond supportive care. The CDC advises staying home, limiting contact with others except for medical care, and waiting to return to normal activities until symptoms are improving overall and there has been no fever for at least 24 hours without fever-reducing medicine.

Prescription antiviral drugs are an option for some patients. They can make illness milder, shorten the time a person is sick, and may reduce some complications. They work best when started early, ideally within two days after symptoms begin, although they may still help later in people who are very sick or at high risk.

People at higher risk for serious complications, or people who feel very sick or are worried about their illness, should contact a health care provider promptly. The CDC specifically calls out higher-risk groups such as people with asthma, diabetes, or heart disease.

Antibiotics do not treat flu because flu is caused by a virus, not bacteria. Taking antibiotics when they are not needed can still cause side effects and other harms.

This document is best used for questions about flu self-care, when antivirals matter, and why antibiotics are not appropriate for routine influenza.
