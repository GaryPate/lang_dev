# Back Pain

Back pain is one of the most common medical problems in the United States. It can range from a dull ache to sudden sharp pain, and it may start suddenly after lifting, a fall, or another injury, or it may build up more slowly over time.

Back pain can be acute, subacute, or chronic. Acute pain often lasts days to a few weeks, subacute pain lasts 4 to 12 weeks, and chronic pain lasts more than 12 weeks.

Symptoms can include pain in one area or more generalized pain, stiffness, pain that gets worse with lifting or bending, pain that gets worse when resting or sitting, pain that radiates to the buttocks or leg, and numbness or weakness in the legs or feet.

Common contributors include muscle strain or sprain, disc problems, spinal stenosis, vertebral fractures, arthritis-related inflammation, osteoporosis, kidney problems, and other medical conditions. Sometimes no single cause is identified.

The source advises medical review if pain does not improve after a few weeks or if it happens with numbness and tingling, severe pain not helped by medication, pain after a fall or injury, trouble urinating, weakness or numbness in the legs, fever, or unintentional weight loss.

This document is useful for general questions about common back-pain patterns and the red flags that should prompt clinician assessment.
