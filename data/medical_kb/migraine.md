Migraine is more than a bad headache. It is an ongoing health condition with recurrent episodes that can include head pain plus other symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, mood change, extreme tiredness, and sensitivity to light, noise, or smells.

Migraine episodes may last from hours to days. A common pattern is moderate to severe throbbing pain, often on one side of the head. Some people have aura symptoms before or during an episode, such as visual changes, trouble speaking, or tingling.

Possible triggers differ from person to person. The source lists examples such as sudden weather changes, too much or too little sleep, strong smells, emotional stress, physical strain, loud noises, low blood sugar or skipped meals, tobacco, alcohol, some medicines, hormonal changes, and bright or flashing lights.

There is no cure, but symptoms can often be managed. Early self-care steps may include resting in a quiet dark room, using a cool cloth or ice pack, and drinking fluids, especially if vomiting is part of the episode. Medicines may be used either to treat an episode quickly or to reduce how often episodes happen.

A headache journal can help identify patterns, triggers, response to medicines, and sleep or food links. This document is useful for educational questions about typical migraine symptoms, aura, trigger tracking, and basic management concepts.
